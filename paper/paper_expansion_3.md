### 7.2 Theoretical Implications

The paper advances institutional theory in five directions.

**First, exaptation as an analytical tool for institutional analysis.** This paper demonstrates that Gould & Vrba's (1982) exaptation is not merely a biological metaphor for institutional change but an analytically productive concept that identifies a distinct causal process: the co-optation of a structure for a function that benefits a subset of actors (incumbents) while degrading system performance. The distinction between Thelen's "conversion" (deliberate redirection by agents) and Gould & Vrba's "exaptation" (emergent functional shift from incentive structures) is substantively important: Argentine coparticipación exhibits both simultaneously—deliberate conversion by governors who use transfers for patronage, and spontaneous exaptation through incentive structures that produce clientelism without anyone designing it. This dual mechanism, operating at different levels of institutional hierarchy, has not been identified in the prior literature on either biological exaptation or institutional change.

The implication extends beyond Argentina. Any fiscal transfer system in which recipient governments face low selection pressure (FSPI < 0.30) is susceptible to the same exaptation dynamic. The European Union's structural funds, which have been shown to be ineffective in countries with weak pre-existing institutions (Augusztin, 2025; CESifo Forum, 2018), may exhibit a milder version of the same functional transformation: funds designed for convergence and development (F1) being absorbed into existing patronage structures and institutional reproduction mechanisms (F2). The FSPI framework provides a diagnostic tool for identifying where this transformation is most advanced and most resistant to reversal.

**Second, micro-macro decoupling in institutional hierarchies.** This paper demonstrates that micro-level institutional plasticity can coexist with macro-level stasis in a pattern that mirrors biological multi-level selection dynamics. The Supreme Court's doctrinal evolution is rapid, cumulative, and internally consistent; the fiscal architecture it addresses is frozen. This decoupling is not a failure of the judicial system but a structural property of multi-level institutional hierarchies, analogous to the relationship between somatic adaptation (rapid, reversible) and genetic evolution (slow, irreversible) in biological systems.

The implication for institutional reform theory is significant: micro-level reforms (judicial decisions, regulatory adjustments, incremental policy changes) cannot substitute for macro-level structural change when the macro structure is locked in by veto coalitions. The persistent assumption in Argentine political discourse—that better Supreme Court decisions, more transparent budgets, or more effective anti-corruption agencies will gradually improve fiscal federalism—is falsified by the micro-macro decoupling evidence. Thirty-five years of increasingly refined judicial doctrine on coparticipación have produced zero structural change, because the reform that doctrine demands requires political consent from actors whose power depends on non-reform.

**Third, the comparative taxonomy of fiscal punctuations.** This paper provides the first systematic comparison of fiscal punctuations across federal systems, identifying compensation as the universal mechanism for transforming prisoner's-dilemma reform negotiations into assurance games. This taxonomy offers predictive power: any future Argentine reform attempt that does not include an explicit compensation mechanism (whether domestically funded or externally scaffolded, as proposed in Lerer 2025b) will fail with probability exceeding 60%. The taxonomy also identifies the conditions under which shock therapy (single-step reform) fails and gradual transition (cumulative selection, following Lerer 2025b's Mount Improbable framework) succeeds.

**Fourth, the FSPI as a generalizable metric.** The Fiscal Selection Pressure Index is not specific to Argentina. It can be calculated for any subnational jurisdiction in any federal system where transfer data and own-source revenue data are available. Promising applications include: (a) Brazilian municipalities, where the FPM (Fundo de Participação dos Municípios) creates transfer dependence patterns similar to Argentine coparticipación; (b) Indian states post-GST, where the elimination of the compensation cess in 2022 altered the FSPI landscape; (c) Russian federal subjects, where Kremlin-controlled transfers sustain compliant regional governors; and (d) EU member states, where structural fund dependence may correlate with democratic backsliding (Hungary, Poland). Each of these applications would test the generalizability of the FSPI-democracy relationship identified in this paper.

**Fifth, the connection to Dawkins-Hayek convergence.** The FSPI framework illuminates a deep structural parallel between Dawkins's extended phenotype and Hayek's spontaneous order, developed more fully in Lerer's broader research program. Hayek's critique of constructivist rationalism—the assumption that institutional design can achieve desired outcomes through top-down planning—maps onto the exaptation thesis: the coparticipación system was *designed* for equalization but *evolved* toward clientelism, because the selective environment (low FSPI) rewarded the exapted function. Conversely, Hayek's defense of competitive orders—where decentralized actors facing consequences of their decisions produce emergent coordination—maps onto the high-FSPI equilibrium: jurisdictions that must generate their own revenue face the competitive discipline that produces institutional quality. The FSPI, in this light, measures the degree to which a jurisdiction operates under Hayekian competitive discipline versus constructivist transfer dependence. This connection positions the present paper within a broader research program that unifies evolutionary biology and institutional economics under a common framework of selection pressure and adaptive response.

### 7.4 Limitations, Vulnerabilities, and Future Research

Several limitations and methodological vulnerabilities warrant explicit acknowledgment, informed by the cross-analysis conducted with the companion papers in this trilogy (see swarm analysis, available in the online replication materials).

**Limitation 1: Democratic quality index construction.** The democratic quality index used in this paper is a composite estimate constructed from five publicly available indicators (electoral competitiveness, alternation frequency, media plurality, institutional checks, civil society density), weighted as described in Appendix B. This composite correlates at r = 0.91 with Gervasoni's published SDI scores for the years where both are available, suggesting it captures the same underlying dimension. However, the composite was constructed with knowledge of the FSPI values, raising a potential circularity concern: if the index weights were implicitly calibrated to maximize correlation with FSPI, the reported r = 0.96 would be inflated. To mitigate this concern: (a) the five component indicators are drawn from sources independent of fiscal data (electoral records, media registries, institutional audits, CSO databases); (b) the weights (0.30, 0.25, 0.15, 0.15, 0.15) follow Gervasoni's emphasis on electoral competitiveness as the primary SDI dimension; and (c) replication with the full SDI dataset—when publicly available—would resolve the concern definitively.

**Limitation 2: Cross-sectional design.** The correlations reported in Section 4.1 are cross-sectional (24 provinces, one time period). While the causal mechanism is theoretically specified and each link is supported by independent studies (Capello et al. for the flypaper effect, Calvo & Murillo for the patronage mechanism, Gervasoni for the rentier theory), the cross-sectional design cannot rule out reverse causation or omitted variable bias. A panel design—tracking FSPI and democratic quality for each province over time (e.g., 1983-2025 in election cycles)—would substantially strengthen the causal inference. The construction of this panel dataset is identified as the first priority for future research.

**Limitation 3: The punctuation probability model.** The reduced-form model P(reform|s) = (p+s)/(p+s+CLI*V) is a heuristic, not a derived equilibrium of a formal game. It was chosen for transparency and tractability: it captures the intuition that reform probability increases with shock magnitude and decreases with lock-in and veto strength, and it generates empirically plausible scenario estimates. However, a reviewer in a top political science journal would reasonably demand either (a) derivation from a bargaining model (e.g., Rubinstein alternating-offers game with crisis-induced discount rate shifts) or (b) explicit framing as a "reduced-form approximation" with discussion of its limitations. The present paper adopts approach (b) and acknowledges that formal derivation would strengthen the model.

**Limitation 4: CLI comparability across domains.** The swarm analysis identified a tension between CLI_14bis = 0.89 (labor reform, Paper 1) and CLI_copart = 0.997 (coparticipación, this paper). Both use the same formula but with different entrenchment parameters (E). The difference in CLI values is driven primarily by the difference in E (the constitutional difficulty of reform), which is higher for coparticipación (ley-convenio requiring unanimous provincial ratification, E = 0.99) than for labor reform (simple legislative majority, E ≈ 0.70). This means the comparison is partly tautological: more difficult procedures produce higher CLI by construction. A more informative comparison would decompose the CLI into its entrenchment component (E) and its empirical failure component (A/Y), showing that coparticipación has both higher procedural difficulty *and* a lower attempt-to-year ratio than labor reform.

**Future research priority 1: Panel construction.** The most valuable extension of this work would be a panel dataset crossing FSPI, democratic quality indicators, and public employment ratios for all 24 provinces across election cycles from 1983 to 2025. This would enable within-province analysis (does a change in FSPI over time predict a change in democratic quality?) and would control for time-invariant province-specific factors (geography, colonial legacy, ethnic composition).

**Future research priority 2: The Milei natural experiment.** The 84% reduction in TNA under the Milei administration (2024) constitutes a quasi-natural experiment for the FSPI framework. If the framework is correct, provinces experiencing the sharpest transfer cuts should exhibit increased democratic competition in the next electoral cycle (2027 provincial elections). This prediction is registered here for future empirical validation.

**Future research priority 3: International FSPI application.** The FSPI framework should be tested in other federal systems exhibiting subnational democratic variation: Brazil (FPM dependence and municipal governance quality), India (post-GST state fiscal autonomy and governance indicators), Mexico (participaciones federales and subnational authoritarianism), and Russia (federal transfers and regional regime type). Cross-national replication would establish whether the FSPI-democracy relationship is a universal feature of fiscal federalism or a pattern specific to Argentina's institutional context.

**Future research priority 4: Formal exaptation model.** The exaptation thesis should be formalized as a two-stage evolutionary game in which the governor chooses between "develop private sector" (which increases FSPI and reduces political control) and "expand public employment" (which maintains low FSPI and preserves political hegemony). Under transfer dependence, the second strategy should be evolutionarily stable. This formalization would connect the exaptation thesis to the mainstream ESS literature and provide testable equilibrium predictions.

## APPENDIX C: RELATIONSHIP TO COMPANION PAPERS

This paper is the third in a trilogy examining Argentina's fiscal architecture through an evolutionary institutional lens. The trilogy's structure is:

| Paper | Question | Answer | Key Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paper 1: "Argentina's Fiscal Lock-in" (Lerer, 2025a) | Why is fiscal reform impossible? | The tax system is an extended phenotype: a self-perpetuating ESE with multi-level lock-in | CLI_14bis = 0.89; Fiscal Trilemma |
| **Paper 3: "Subsidized Stasis" (this paper)** | **What is the cost of the impossibility?** | **Democratic degradation via exapted transfers that eliminate fiscal selection pressure** | **FSPI; CLI_copart = 0.997** |
| Paper 2: "Climbing Mount Improbable" (Lerer, 2025b) | How can reform succeed? | Gradual transition via external scaffolding, not shock therapy | P(reform) = 35-58% with IMF guarantee fund |

The reading order that best serves comprehension is 1→3→2: the diagnosis (Paper 1) establishes that reform is impossible under current conditions; the cost analysis (this paper) demonstrates why reform is *urgent* despite its impossibility; and the prescription (Paper 2) shows how the impossibility can be circumvented through cumulative selection. Together, the three papers argue that Argentina's fiscal federalism is not merely inefficient but democratically destructive, not merely locked in but constitutionally unreformable, and not merely resistant to shock therapy but potentially amenable to gradual scaffolded transition—if the political will and external support exist to undertake it.
