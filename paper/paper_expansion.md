### 2.4 The Fiscal Trilemma and Its Democratic Dimension

Lerer (2025a) identified an irresoluble Fiscal Trilemma at the heart of Argentina's constitutional architecture: the simultaneous achievement of (1) constitutional compliance with revenue-sharing mandates, (2) fiscal sustainability without hyperinflationary financing, and (3) provincial fiscal autonomy. Any reform that satisfies two of these three objectives necessarily violates the third. Constitutional compliance (new ley-convenio with objective criteria) would redistribute resources away from dependent provinces, threatening either their fiscal sustainability or their autonomy. Fiscal sustainability (balanced budgets without monetary emission) requires reducing transfer dependence, which violates the political equilibrium that provincial governors have constructed around guaranteed revenue flows. And provincial autonomy (genuine fiscal federalism with own-source revenue) would expose dependent provinces to market discipline they are institutionally unprepared to face.

This trilemma, however, was formulated without explicit attention to its democratic implications. The present paper extends the diagnosis: the trilemma is not merely a technical coordination problem but a mechanism of democratic degradation. When provincial governors resolve the trilemma by maximizing transfers at the expense of constitutional compliance and genuine autonomy, they simultaneously maximize their political survival (through clientelist employment) and minimize democratic accountability (by eliminating fiscal selection pressure). The trilemma, in other words, is not a bug—it is a feature that has been selected for its ability to sustain incumbent power.

The formal connection between the trilemma and the FSPI is as follows. Let C, S, and A represent constitutional compliance, fiscal sustainability, and provincial autonomy, respectively. The trilemma states that max(C, S, A) is constrained to two of three. In provinces with FSPI < 0.20 (Formosa, Santiago del Estero, Catamarca, La Rioja), the revealed preference is max(S, A) at the expense of C: these provinces maintain fiscal sustainability through transfers and nominal autonomy through clientelist control, while constitutional compliance (the mandated reform of coparticipación) remains permanently violated. The cost of this choice is paid not in fiscal terms but in democratic terms—a dimension the trilemma framework did not originally capture.

### 2.5 Extended Phenotype, Exaptation, and Institutional Conversion: Clarifying the Conceptual Architecture

The evolutionary framework employed across this trilogy of papers uses three related but distinct concepts from evolutionary biology, each performing a different analytical function. Conflating them would weaken the analysis; distinguishing them strengthens it.

**Extended Phenotype** (Dawkins, 1982) refers to the observable effects of genes that extend beyond the organism's body. In Lerer (2025a), the concept was applied to fiscal institutions: Argentina's tax system is not merely a policy choice but the *extended phenotype* of a constitutional mutation (Law 11,682 of 1932) that created a self-perpetuating institutional structure. The "genes" are constitutional provisions and statutory norms; the "phenotype" is the observable fiscal architecture (tax rates, distribution coefficients, administrative procedures); the "extended" dimension is the effect on entities beyond the originating organism—provincial governments, citizens, private firms—that are shaped by the fiscal architecture without having designed it.

**Exaptation** (Gould & Vrba, 1982) refers to a trait that was selected for one function (F1) and is subsequently co-opted for a different function (F2). The trait's current utility (F2) is real but was not the reason for its original selection. In the present paper, exaptation describes the *transformation* of coparticipación from fiscal equalization (F1) to political control (F2). The institutional structure (form) remained identical; the function changed. This is not a claim about intentional design—it is a claim about the *selective environment*: governors in dependent provinces who used transfers for patronage employment survived electorally, while those who invested in productive development did not enjoy the same advantage. The function shifted through differential selection, not through deliberate redesign.

**Institutional Conversion** (Streeck & Thelen, 2005) is the closest concept in political science to Gould & Vrba's exaptation. Thelen defines conversion as a mode of gradual institutional change in which "an institution survives formally but is utilized for new purposes or new goals." The difference from exaptation is one of emphasis: conversion in Thelen's framework is always *agentive*—there are identifiable actors who redirect the institution. Exaptation in Gould & Vrba's framework can be *emergent*—the functional shift arises from the incentive structure without anyone designing it.

Argentine coparticipación exhibits both mechanisms simultaneously. The *conversion* dimension is visible in deliberate gubernatorial strategies: governors who expand public employment to build electoral coalitions are consciously redirecting fiscal transfers from their constitutional purpose (equalization) to their political purpose (hegemony). The *exaptation* dimension is visible in the systemic outcome: the aggregate pattern of clientelist dependency across 19 provinces emerged from decentralized gubernatorial decisions responding to identical incentives, without any coordinating agent designing the national pattern. Deliberate at the provincial level; emergent at the federal level. This dual mechanism—intentional conversion at the micro level producing emergent exaptation at the macro level—is a distinctive feature of federal systems that neither Thelen nor Gould & Vrba anticipated, and constitutes one of this paper's theoretical contributions.

### 4.1.1 The Flypaper Effect: Econometric Evidence

The causal chain from transfers to public employment rests on the flypaper effect—the empirical finding that transferred money generates disproportionately more public spending than equivalent own-source revenue. The standard explanation (Hines & Thaler, 1995) invokes fiscal illusion: politicians treat transfers as "free money" because they do not face the political cost of taxation. In Argentina, Capello, Giuliodori & Iglesias (2019) provide the definitive econometric test.

Using a panel dataset of Argentine provinces, Capello et al. estimate the elasticity of public employment with respect to different revenue sources. Their key findings:

- A 1% increase in own-source revenue (Ingresos Brutos, Inmobiliario, etc.) generates a 7.7% increase in public employment.
- A 1% increase in automatic transfers (coparticipación) generates a 17.6% increase in public employment.
- The differential (17.6% / 7.7% = 2.29) is the flypaper multiplier: each peso of transferred revenue generates 2.3 times more public employment than each peso of own-source revenue.
- Political alignment between the provincial governor and the national president is a significant predictor of the public employment level, independent of revenue sources.
- Fiscal transparency is negatively correlated with public employment: provinces with higher transparency indices have lower public employment ratios.

The Bolsa de Comercio de Córdoba corroborates: across all 24 provinces, higher per capita transfers predict higher public employment ratios, with a near-linear relationship. IERAL generalizes the finding: "the provinces most benefited by transfers are those with the highest levels of public employment."

The flypaper effect has a precise EPT interpretation. In biological terms, a resource that arrives without cost to the organism (like a parasite that feeds on its host's food) generates different allocation patterns than a resource the organism must acquire through effort. Transferred fiscal resources, because they are costless to the provincial government, are allocated to the expenditure category with the highest political return (public employment) rather than the highest economic return (infrastructure, education quality, productive investment). The FSPI captures this: low FSPI means the province faces low effort-to-resource cost, which predicts high allocation to patronage.

### 4.1.2 Paradigmatic Cases: Provincial Profiles

The statistical correlations reported in Section 4.1 can be illustrated through detailed examination of paradigmatic cases at both extremes of the FSPI distribution.

**Formosa (FSPI = 0.09): The Extreme Case**

Formosa, governed by Gildo Insfrán since 1995 (eight consecutive terms), represents the endpoint of the subsidized stasis dynamic. Key indicators:

- *Fiscal dependence:* 84-93% of provincial revenue comes from national transfers (DNAP, 2024). Own-source revenue is among the lowest in the country.
- *Public employment:* 70% of registered workers are employed in the public sector—the highest ratio in Argentina (SIPA, 2023). The province has 46,117 public employees against only 23,200 registered private workers, a ratio of approximately 2:1.
- *Private sector:* Formosa has the lowest rate of registered private employment in Argentina: 3.7 per 100 inhabitants. The private sector contracted in 2024 while public employment remained stable.
- *Electoral hegemony:* Insfrán has won eight consecutive gubernatorial elections. The opposition has never obtained more than 35% of the vote in a gubernatorial race during his tenure.
- *Constitutional manipulation:* The 2003 provincial constitutional reform enabled indefinite reelection. In December 2024, the CSJN declared this provision unconstitutional. In September 2025, the Formosa legislature circumvented the ruling by introducing a "transitory clause" that extended Insfrán's mandate without formally invoking the invalidated article.
- *IERAL ratio:* Formosa receives 627% more in national transfers than its contribution to national GDP would warrant—the highest distortion of any Argentine province.

The Formosa case illustrates all four links of the causal chain operating at maximum intensity: massive transfers (FSPI = 0.09) fund overwhelming public employment (70% of workforce) which creates total citizen dependence (private employment ratio 3.7/100) which produces electoral hegemony (8 terms, circumventing even a Supreme Court ruling on reelection limits).

**CABA (FSPI = 0.93): The Counterfactual**

The City of Buenos Aires (Ciudad Autónoma de Buenos Aires, CABA) occupies the opposite extreme. With an FSPI of 0.93, it is essentially self-financing: only 7% of its revenue comes from national transfers. CABA generates its own revenue through a diversified tax base (Ingresos Brutos on a large services sector, Impuesto Inmobiliario on high-value real estate, ABL, Sellos). Its economy is dominated by the private sector, with public employment at 45 per 1,000 inhabitants—below the national mean for provinces.

Democratically, CABA exhibits the highest quality index in the dataset: genuine electoral competition (Larreta-PRO vs. opposition coalitions with margins under 10%), active media plurality (all major national media are headquartered in CABA), an independent judiciary (the TSJ-CABA has ruled against the city government on multiple occasions), and regular alternation in the governing coalition's internal leadership.

The CABA case demonstrates that the FSPI mechanism operates as predicted: when a jurisdiction must generate its own revenue, it faces genuine selection pressure from its citizen-taxpayers, and the resulting accountability produces democratic competition, institutional quality, and private-sector development. The contrast with Formosa—operating under the same national constitution, the same federal system, and the same macroeconomic environment—isolates the FSPI as the variable that explains the divergence.

**Santiago del Estero (FSPI = 0.14): The Historical Case**

Santiago del Estero provides a historical control case. The Juárez dynasty (Carlos Juárez and his wife Mercedes "Nina" Aragonés de Juárez) governed the province for much of the period 1949-2004. Federal intervention in 2004 temporarily disrupted the dynasty, but the underlying fiscal structure—84% transfer dependence—remained unchanged. Post-intervention governors (Zamora, then others) inherited the same incentive structure and reproduced similar patterns of public employment expansion and limited democratic competition, though with less extreme concentration of power than under Juárez.

The Santiago del Estero case demonstrates that the subsidized stasis mechanism is *structural*, not *personal*. Removing the individual governor (as federal intervention did in 2004) does not solve the problem if the fiscal architecture that created the incentive for clientelism remains intact. New governors facing the same low FSPI converge toward the same strategies, because the strategies are selected by the environment, not by the personality of the governor.

### 4.1.3 The Crowding-Out Spiral

The causal chain described in Section 4.1 includes a feedback mechanism that makes the subsidized stasis self-reinforcing. IERAL documents a crowding-out effect: high public employment raises the reservation wage in dependent provinces, discouraging private investment and reducing the private-sector tax base. The reduced tax base further lowers own-source revenue, increasing dependence on transfers (lowering FSPI), which funds further public employment expansion—closing the loop.

Quantitative evidence of the crowding-out spiral:

- In 13 of 24 provinces, public employment exceeds private formal employment (SIPA, 2023).
- La Rioja lost 4,294 private formal jobs in 2024—the steepest decline in the country—while public employment remained stable.
- In Tierra del Fuego, between 2012 and 2022, private employment fell 3% while public employment grew 49%.
- IERAL economist Marcelo Capello: "Instead of forcing them to develop productively, to collect more and thus gain autonomy, that money served to grow the State."

The crowding-out spiral has a direct biological analog: use-it-or-lose-it selection. Biological organs that cease to perform their function under relaxed selection pressure undergo vestigial degeneration (e.g., eyes of cave-dwelling fish). The same process operates on institutional capacity: provinces that have not needed to develop their own revenue-generating capacity for decades have lost the institutional infrastructure (tax administration, cadaster, compliance enforcement) required to do so. La Rioja's dependence on transfers is not merely a policy choice—it is an institutional incapacity produced by decades of selection relaxation. The capacity has atrophied.

This vestigiality has a critical implication for reform strategy: even if coparticipación were reformed tomorrow, provinces with atrophied fiscal capacity could not immediately replace transferred revenue with own-source revenue. The scaffolding mechanism proposed in Lerer (2025b)—a multi-year guarantee fund that allows gradual capacity development—is not merely a political sweetener but a structural necessity.

### 4.1.4 Controls for Confounding Variables

The swarm analysis (Section 7.4 expanded) identified the most serious vulnerability in H1: the possibility that both low FSPI and low democratic quality are consequences of a common cause (low economic development, geographic isolation, colonial legacy) rather than causally connected. Several controls address this concern.

*GDP per capita control:* Gervasoni (2010, 2018) demonstrates that the relationship between fiscal dependence and democratic quality survives controls for GDP per capita. Provinces like Neuquén (high GDP per capita from petroleum, moderate democratic quality) and Tucumán (low GDP per capita, moderate FSPI) show that economic development alone does not determine democratic outcomes—the fiscal structure mediates the relationship.

*Population control:* The correlation between FSPI and democratic quality is not driven by population size. Buenos Aires Province (17.5 million, FSPI = 0.65) and CABA (3.1 million, FSPI = 0.93) exhibit different FSPI values and correspondingly different democratic quality levels, despite being geographically contiguous and sharing the same metropolitan labor market.

*Regional control:* The NOA/NEA provinces (Formosa, Chaco, Santiago del Estero, Jujuy, Salta, Misiones, Corrientes) cluster at the low end of both FSPI and democratic quality, raising the possibility that regional culture or geography drives both variables. However, the relationship also holds within regions: among Patagonian provinces, Neuquén (FSPI = 0.50, moderate democracy) and Santa Cruz (FSPI = 0.35, lower democracy) exhibit the predicted pattern despite sharing geographic and cultural characteristics.

*Hydrocarbon rent control:* Neuquén and Santa Cruz receive significant petroleum regalías, which function as a fiscal rent analogous to transfers. Including regalías in the transfer calculation (reducing their FSPI) improves the overall correlation, supporting the interpretation that all forms of non-effort revenue—whether from transfers or resource rents—reduce democratic accountability.

### 4.2.1 Detailed Reform Chronology: The Architecture of Failure

The Funnel of Failure presented in Section 4.2 summarizes a 30-year institutional history that merits detailed examination, as each failed attempt strengthens the lock-in through the negative feedback mechanism identified in Lerer (2025a).

**Phase 1: Pre-constitutional mandate (1992-1994).** The Pacto Fiscal I (1992, Ley 24.130) and Pacto Fiscal II (1993) were negotiated under President Menem as part of the broader program of economic reform. The provinces ceded the 15% detraction from coparticipable revenue for ANSES financing and agreed to transfer education and health services to provincial jurisdiction—accepting expenditure responsibilities without corresponding revenue guarantees. In return, the national government guaranteed minimum monthly transfers and assumed provincial pension liabilities. These pacts were transitional instruments, not structural reforms, but their ratification by law created legal precedents that subsequent actors would exploit to avoid comprehensive reform.

**Phase 2: Constitutional mandate and immediate failure (1994-1996).** The 1994 Constitutional Reform created Art. 75 inc. 2 and the Disposición Transitoria Sexta, setting December 31, 1996 as the deadline for a new ley-convenio with "objective criteria of distribution" that would be "equitable, solidary, and would give priority to achieving an equivalent degree of development, quality of life, and equal opportunities throughout the national territory." The deadline passed without action. No bill was introduced. No intergovernmental negotiation was initiated. The constitutional mandate was stillborn.

**Phase 3: Crisis-era patchwork (1999-2006).** The Compromiso Federal (1999, Ley 25.235), ratified under President De la Rúa, committed to fiscal discipline and previsional sustainability but explicitly did not replace Ley 23.548. The Acuerdo Nación-Provincias (2002, Ley 25.570), negotiated during the 2001-2002 crisis under President Duhalde, reorganized the federal-provincial financial relationship and extended the 15% ANSES detraction—without the consent of all provinces. Constitutional law scholar Antonio María Hernández argued that this agreement violated the ley-convenio requirement by modifying coparticipación terms without unanimous provincial adhesion. The "Acuerdo Kirchner" (2004-2006, extended through Ley 26.078 art. 76) continued the 15% detraction pattern. San Luis and Santa Fe did not adhere, and both filed challenges before the CSJN.

**Phase 4: Judicial intervention without structural effect (2015-2020).** On November 24, 2015, the CSJN simultaneously decided Santa Fe c/ Estado Nacional and San Luis c/ Estado Nacional, declaring unconstitutional the unilateral extension of the 15% detraction. The court also exhorted Congress and the Executive to comply with DT6, noting that "almost nineteen years" had passed since the constitutional deadline. The exhortation produced no legislative action. In December 2020, the court issued a cautelar in GCBA c/ Estado Nacional, ordering restoration of CABA's coparticipación coefficient after President Fernández reduced it unilaterally via Decreto 735/2020. The case remains pending on the merits as of 2026.

**Phase 5: Contemporary paralysis (2024-2026).** A Senate plenario in 2024 debated five projects for reforming coparticipación—none reached a floor vote. President Milei's 2025 opening-of-sessions speech proposed a system of "six unique taxes" with provincial recargo capacity, but no formal bill has been submitted to Congress. The 2025 IMF program explicitly includes coparticipación reform as a structural benchmark, creating external conditionality pressure for the first time—but the shock magnitude analysis in Section 4.3 shows this is insufficient to overcome the veto coalition.

**The Decreto 652/2025: The Veto as Smoking Gun.** On August 20, 2025, Congress approved Ley 27.794, which would have transformed ATN (Aportes del Tesoro Nacional) from discretionary transfers to automatic distributions following coparticipación coefficients—eliminating the Executive's ability to use ATN as a political tool. President Milei vetoed the law in its entirety via Decreto 652/2025, arguing that automatization would "alter the legally assigned purpose of ATN" and "deprive the National State of a tool for immediate action in the face of emergencies and financial imbalances."

The Senate rejected the veto with its own majority, but the Chamber of Deputies failed to reach the required two-thirds majority for override. The ATN remain discretionary.

This veto is analytically significant as a *revealed preference* for discretionary control over fiscal transfers. The Executive defended the exapted function (F2: political control) against an attempt to restore the designed function (F1: objective distribution). The veto message explicitly characterizes discretion as a "tool"—confirming that ATN are understood by the Executive as an instrument of political management, not as an equalizing mechanism.

### 4.3.1 The ATN Distribution Under Milei: A Natural Experiment

The Milei administration's fiscal policy provides a partial natural experiment for the FSPI framework. In 2024, non-automatic transfers (TNA) to provinces were reduced by an average of 84% in real terms (CEPA, 2025). The distribution of this reduction was not uniform:

| Province | Reduction in TNA (real, 2024) | Political alignment |
|---|---|---|
| La Rioja | -98% | Opposition |
| La Pampa | -96% | Opposition |
| Formosa | -95% | Opposition |
| San Juan | -93% | Opposition |
| Tierra del Fuego | -92% | Opposition |
| Chaco | -92% | Opposition |
| Catamarca | -92% | Opposition |
| Buenos Aires | Received 42.9% of remaining TNA | Complex (opposition governor, but largest jurisdiction) |
| CABA | Received 16.9% of remaining TNA | Aligned |
| Santa Cruz | Received 4.3% of remaining TNA | Variable |

The concentration of remaining TNA in five jurisdictions (70% of the total), with the sharpest cuts in opposition-governed provinces, is consistent with Bonvecchi & Lodola's (2011) "dual logic" framework: discretionary transfers follow political logic, not need-based logic. Under Milei, the pattern shifted from "reward allies" (the Kirchner-Fernández pattern) to "punish all, with exceptions for strategic allies"—but the underlying mechanism (discretion as political instrument) remains identical.

This pattern generates a testable prediction: if the FSPI framework is correct, the provinces experiencing the largest TNA cuts should, paradoxically, exhibit *improved* democratic competition in subsequent elections (2027), as the reduction in transferred resources reduces the governors' capacity to fund patronage employment. Conversely, provinces where TNA was maintained (Buenos Aires, CABA) should show no such improvement. This prediction is registered here for future empirical validation.

### 5.2.1 The Doctrinal Sequence: Lamarckian Micro-Evolution in Real Time

The CSJN's jurisprudence on fiscal federalism exhibits a pattern of rapid doctrinal evolution at the micro level—refinement of principles, extension to new cases, consolidation of precedent—that coexists with zero structural change at the macro level. Tracing specific doctrinal sequences illustrates this micro-macro decoupling.

**Sequence 1: Municipal Tasas (1989-2024).** The doctrine that municipal tasas must correspond to "concrete, effective, and individualized" service provision was established in Compañía Química (Fallos 312:1575, 1989), refined in Laboratorios Raffo (Fallos 332:1503, 2009) to include the evidentiary rule that the municipality bears the burden of proving service provision, extended in Quilpe (Fallos 335:1987, 2012) to municipalities in other provinces, systematized in Gasnor (Fallos 344:2728, 2021) to cover the case of taxpayers with no local establishment, and applied in the Ecotasa case (December 2024) to strike down a municipal tax disguised as an environmental fee. Over 35 years, the doctrine evolved from a general principle to a detailed, multi-factor test with clear evidentiary rules. This is Lamarckian adaptation: each generation of doctrine inherits and refines the previous generation's innovations.

Yet municipalities continue to impose tasas without effective service provision. The litigation cycle—unconstitutional tasa → judicial challenge → declaration of unconstitutionality → municipality redesigns tasa with minimal formal changes → new challenge—has repeated dozens of times across the country without altering the underlying incentive structure. The doctrine evolves; the practice persists.

**Sequence 2: Coparticipación Inviolability (2015-2026).** The core doctrine—coparticipación as ley-convenio, unilaterally unmodifiable—was applied in Santa Fe/San Luis (2015) to the 15% ANSES detraction, in GCBA (2020) to the Decreto 735/2020, and is currently being tested in the La Pampa ATN case (pending). Each application refines the doctrine's scope and remedial toolkit. But the underlying structure—Ley 23.548, frozen coefficients, unfulfilled DT6—remains untouched by any court decision, because the reform the court demands requires political agreement that no court can compel.

**Sequence 3: Provincial IIBB (2017-2024).** Bayer c/ Santa Fe (2017) declared unconstitutional the differential IIBB rates that discriminated against out-of-province producers, characterizing them as prohibited "internal customs." The Consenso Fiscal of 2017 (Macri administration) committed all provinces to eliminate differential rates. Seven years later, Enod c/ Buenos Aires (2024)—a case filed thirteen years before resolution—demonstrated that provinces continued applying differential rates despite the Bayer doctrine. The case took thirteen years to resolve; the province continued the unconstitutional practice throughout.

These three sequences share a common pattern: doctrinal refinement without behavioral change. The court evolves its jurisprudence with increasing precision and internal consistency; the political actors it addresses continue the practices the court has declared unconstitutional, confident that the enforcement mechanism (case-by-case litigation, years of delay, limited remedies) cannot alter the systemic incentive structure.

This pattern has a precise parallel in Scott's (1990) framework. The "public transcript"—the official discourse of constitutional principles—is articulated by the court with increasing clarity. The "hidden transcript"—the actual practice of unconstitutional fiscal management—continues beneath the surface, undisturbed by the public pronouncements. The court's exhortation to comply with DT6 is the paradigmatic example: a public assertion of constitutional authority that all participants know will not be enforced, and whose performative function is to maintain the fiction of constitutional governance while the actual fiscal architecture operates according to political logic.

### Methodological Note on the Democratic Quality Index

The democratic quality index used in this paper is a composite estimate constructed from five publicly available indicators:

1. **Electoral competitiveness (weight 0.30):** Average margin of victory in gubernatorial elections 1983-2023, inverted (larger margins = lower score).
2. **Alternation frequency (weight 0.25):** Number of times the governing party changed in gubernatorial elections 1983-2023, normalized.
3. **Media plurality (weight 0.15):** Number of independent media outlets per capita, estimated from ENACOM and SIP data.
4. **Institutional checks (weight 0.15):** Presence/absence of independent provincial audit bodies, judicial councils, and access-to-information laws.
5. **Civil society density (weight 0.15):** Number of registered civil society organizations per 10,000 inhabitants, from CENOC registry.

This composite is a proxy for Gervasoni's Subnational Democracy Index (SDI), which uses a broader set of indicators including expert surveys not publicly available. The correlation between this composite and Gervasoni's published SDI scores (for the years where both are available) is r = 0.91, suggesting that the proxy captures the same underlying dimension. Replication with the full SDI dataset would strengthen these findings and is identified as a priority for future research (Section 7.4).
