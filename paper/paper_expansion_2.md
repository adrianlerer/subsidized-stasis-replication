### 5.1.1 The ATN as Exaptation Laboratory: Distributional Evidence

The Aportes del Tesoro Nacional (ATN) constitute the purest empirical manifestation of the exaptation thesis. Created by Art. 5 of Ley 23.548 with the declared purpose of "attending emergency situations and financial imbalances of provincial governments," ATN were designed as a counter-cyclical fiscal tool (F1). In practice, they function as the Executive's primary instrument of political management over governors (F2).

The evidence for this functional transformation is both qualitative and quantitative.

**Qualitative evidence: the "dual logic."** Bonvecchi & Lodola (2011) provide the foundational analysis. Their theory of intergovernmental transfers identifies two simultaneous logics operating in ATN distribution: an economic logic (criteria of equity, compensation for imbalances) and a political logic (maximization of legislative support and territorial control). Using data on ATN and other discretionary funds, they demonstrate that the contribution of each province to the formation of the presidential legislative bloc is a better predictor of geographic distribution than socioeconomic indicators. The political return on a transfer is not proportional to its economic magnitude: small, targeted transfers to overrepresented provinces with compliant governors produce disproportionate legislative returns.

Simison (2018) extends the analysis to the 1990s and 2000s, finding that the Executive exploits ATN discretionality according to coalition needs, not fiscal necessity. Ronconi & Zarazaga (2019), through 120 interviews with Peronist brokers, confirm that social programs with discretionary allocation (such as Argentina Trabaja) are distributed according to the number of voters per household in targeted neighborhoods, not according to formal eligibility criteria. The mechanism operates at multiple scales: the national Executive uses ATN to discipline governors; governors use patronage employment to discipline citizens; brokers use micro-transfers to discipline individual voters. Each level replicates the exaptation pattern: a formally equalizing mechanism is operationally a control mechanism.

**Quantitative evidence: the Milei cuts as revealed preference.** The Milei administration's 2024 fiscal policy provides the starkest quantitative evidence of ATN politicization. CEPA (2025) documents that non-automatic transfers fell by an average of 84% in real terms. But the distribution of the remaining 16% was highly concentrated: Buenos Aires received 42.9%, CABA 16.9%, Santa Cruz 4.3%, Misiones 3.4%, and Mendoza 3.1%—five jurisdictions absorbing 70% of the remaining TNA. Six provinces that had received transfers in 2023 showed zero execution in 2024. The provinces with the deepest cuts (La Rioja -98%, La Pampa -96%, Formosa -95%) are all governed by opposition parties.

The OPC (Oficina de Presupuesto del Congreso) confirms the pattern: in the first four months of 2024, many provinces showed near-zero execution in ATN-related budget lines (Relaciones con las Provincias y Desarrollo Regional), FONID (education transfers), and saneamiento. The pattern continued into 2025, with occasional exceptions for specific provinces at specific moments (Tucumán, Santa Cruz, Tierra del Fuego, Entre Ríos), suggesting political rather than technical criteria for disbursement timing.

The interpretive significance of the Milei cuts is not that they represent a new phenomenon—every administration has used ATN politically—but that the magnitude of the cuts makes the political logic visible in a way that marginal adjustments do not. When the ATN pie is large, political allocation can be masked by across-the-board distribution with marginal differentiation. When the pie shrinks by 84%, the remaining distribution reveals the underlying political function with clarity.

**The veto as institutional defense of exaptation.** The Decreto 652/2025 vetoing Ley 27.794 (automatization of ATN) completes the evidence. The veto message explicitly defends discretion as a "tool for immediate action"—language that confirms ATN are understood by the Executive as an instrument, not as an entitlement. The Executive's argument—that automatization would "denaturalize the purpose" of ATN—is analytically revealing: the "purpose" being defended is the exapted function (F2: political control), not the designed function (F1: emergency equalization). The Senate rejected the veto with its own majority, but Diputados failed to override, leaving the discretionary mechanism intact.

This sequence—congressional attempt to restore F1 (automatization), executive defense of F2 (discretion), legislative failure to override—is the institutional equivalent of an organism defending an exapted trait against reversal. The coparticipación system has developed institutional antibodies against de-exaptation.

### 5.1.2 The Distinction Between Exaptation and Corruption

A potential objection to the exaptation thesis is that it describes ordinary political corruption under a needlessly biological label. This objection misses a critical distinction.

Corruption is individual deviation from institutional norms: a governor who diverts public funds to personal accounts violates the law and can, in principle, be prosecuted and replaced. The institution survives the corrupt individual; the pathology is personal.

Exaptation is systemic transformation of institutional function: the institution itself operates according to a logic different from its design, and the actors within it are *responding rationally to incentive structures* that reward the exapted function. Insfrán in Formosa is not "corrupt" in the traditional sense—he is not stealing public funds. He is allocating them to public employment, which is legal, and using the resulting electoral advantage to maintain power, which is constitutional (until the 2024 CSJN ruling on reelección indefinida, which he subsequently circumvented through a legal mechanism). The pathology is not individual but structural: any governor facing the same incentive structure (FSPI = 0.09, 70% public workforce, no private sector alternative) would converge toward the same strategy, because the strategy is *selected* by the fiscal environment.

This distinction has direct policy implications. Anti-corruption measures (transparency laws, judicial oversight, media exposure) target individual deviation and are irrelevant to exaptation. Structural reform (changing the fiscal architecture that creates the incentive for exaptation) is the only remedy, and it is precisely what the veto coalition blocks. The exaptation framework explains why decades of anti-corruption discourse have not improved democratic quality in dependent provinces: the diagnosis targets the symptom (individual misbehavior), not the disease (institutional functional shift).

### 6.1.1 The Compensation Imperative: Mechanism Design Analysis

The three successful fiscal punctuations share a common mechanism design feature that deserves formal analysis: the transformation of a prisoner's dilemma (PD) into an assurance game (AG) through credible compensation commitments.

In the absence of compensation, coparticipación reform is a prisoner's dilemma for net-receiver provinces. Each receiver province faces a choice between supporting reform (which may increase long-term efficiency but reduces guaranteed short-term revenue) and blocking reform (which preserves guaranteed revenue). The dominant strategy is to block, regardless of what other provinces do, because the downside risk of reform (immediate revenue loss) is certain while the upside (long-term efficiency gain) is uncertain and delayed.

India's GST Council solved this PD through the compensation cess mechanism: states were guaranteed 14% annual revenue growth for five years, funded by a dedicated cess on luxury goods. This guarantee converted the game from PD to AG: states could support reform knowing that their revenue would not fall, and the external funding source (the cess) made the commitment credible because it did not depend on political decisions by the central government. By 2022, post-scaffolding GST revenue exceeded pre-GST baselines for all states, validating the guarantee mechanism and demonstrating that the reform was self-sustaining after the compensation period ended.

Brazil's EC 132 used a similar mechanism: the ICMS compensation fund (2029-2032) guarantees revenue to states that lose from the elimination of the "guerra fiscal" (competitive state tax incentives). The fund's constitutional status (embedded in the amendment itself) provides a credibility anchor that ordinary legislation would not.

Argentina has no equivalent mechanism. The Milei proposal (six unified taxes with provincial recargo) implies a fundamental restructuring of revenue sources but includes no compensation fund, no transition period guarantee, and no external credibility anchor. The Lerer (2025b) proposal for a USD 15 billion IMF/IDB guarantee fund is, to date, the only articulated compensation mechanism for Argentine fiscal reform. The present paper's scenario analysis (Section 4.3) confirms that without such a mechanism, reform probability remains below 50% under any politically plausible scenario.

### 6.3.1 The Senate Problem: Why Argentina's Veto Is Unreformable

The most distinctive feature of Argentina's fiscal stasis, compared to the three benchmark cases, is the constitutional embedding of the veto coalition in the Senate's territorial representation. Art. 54 CN assigns three senators to each province regardless of population. This means:

- Formosa (population 600,000) has the same senatorial representation as Buenos Aires Province (population 17.5 million)—a representational ratio of approximately 29:1.
- The 19 net-receiver provinces (combined population ~16 million) control 57 of 72 Senate seats. The 5 net-giver provinces (combined population ~30 million) control only 15 seats.
- Any ley-convenio requires absolute majority in both chambers. In the Senate, the net-receiver coalition holds a structural supermajority (79%) that no electoral result can alter, because the representational formula is constitutionally fixed.

This is not analogous to the German Bundesrat (where Länder representation is proportional to population, with a minimum floor) or to the Indian Rajya Sabha (where state representation is roughly proportional to population). Argentina's Senate is the most malapportioned upper chamber among major federal democracies, and this malapportionment is constitutionally entrenched: changing Art. 54 requires a constitutional convention (Art. 30 CN), which itself requires two-thirds of both chambers—including the Senate whose malapportionment would be the target of reform.

The circularity is complete: the veto coalition that blocks coparticipación reform is the same coalition that would need to approve the constitutional reform required to change the veto structure. No external mechanism—not IMF conditionality, not judicial exhortation, not presidential initiative—can circumvent this constitutional circle. Only a crisis of sufficient magnitude to make the entire constitutional order negotiable (a 2001-level or greater disruption) could create the conditions for simultaneous reform of Senate representation and coparticipación—and even then, the historical precedent (the 1994 reform) shows that constitutional conventions tend to *reinforce* provincial power rather than reduce it, because provincial governors control the selection of convencionales constituyentes.

This is why the scenario analysis in Section 4.3 identifies the minimum shock threshold at 0.689—equivalent to hyperinflationary crisis. Below this threshold, the structural veto coalition remains intact regardless of reform pressure, external conditionality, or presidential commitment.
